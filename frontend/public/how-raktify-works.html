How Raktify works

Explained from zero — for someone who has never heard of a platform like this before.

When a hospital needs blood, Raktify finds it — first from blood-bank fridges, then from living donors — automatically, in minutes instead of hours.

The problem today (without Raktify)

A patient needs blood. The hospital's blood bank doesn't have that group. The family starts running — calling relatives, posting in WhatsApp groups, begging strangers outside blood banks. Nobody knows which fridge in the city actually has the blood, or which healthy donor is 2 km away and willing. Hours pass. Sometimes the patient doesn't have hours.

Raktify replaces that panic with a system. Everything below explains how.

The big picture Two worlds: peace time and emergency time

The secret of Raktify is that it runs two separate machines:

🌱 Peace time — quietly, every day: people sign up as donors, camps collect blood, fridges get stocked. Nothing urgent. This is preparation.

🚨 Emergency time — when a hospital asks: check the fridges first, wake donors only if fridges can't cover it, deliver.

Emergency time is fast only because peace time already did the hard work. That is the whole idea of the platform in one sentence.

One line to remember: peace time fills the tank; emergency time empties it carefully. Six small flows below explain each piece — with the flow number they hand over to.

Flow 1 — Becoming a donor How does an ordinary person enter the system?

Anyone can raise their hand through four doors. But raising your hand does not make you a donor — it only means "I'm willing". Raktify will never call an unverified person for an emergency, because nobody has proof of their blood group or health yet.

One line to remember: signing up = raising your hand. Only the blood bank's lab report (②) makes you a donor. Hands over to: Flow 5 (the testing gate) and Flow 3 (the pool of people who can be called).

Flow 2 — The hospital asks What happens the moment a patient needs blood?

A doctor decides the patient needs blood. Hospital staff fill one form on Raktify: blood group, how many units, needed by when, and how urgent. The moment they press submit, three things happen in the same second — automatically, before any human moves.

One line to remember: one form, three automatic reactions, zero phone calls by the family. Hands over to: Flow 3 (fridges) with the clock from Flow 4 already running.

Flow 3 — Fridges first Why disturb a donor if the blood already exists in a fridge?

Raktify knows what every blood bank's fridge holds. But here is the platform's most important manner: it never takes blood from a fridge — it asks. Like asking a shopkeeper "do you have this?", never reaching behind the counter yourself. Each blood bank with matching stock sees the request on its screen and chooses its own answer.

Bank's answerEffect on this requestEffect on donor routing (Flow 5)

One line to remember: Raktify asks, never takes. The banks stay in control of their own fridges — that is why they trust the platform. Hands over to: Flow 7 if covered, Flow 4 if not.

Flow 4 — Waking the donors Who gets the message, and why not everyone?

Donors are the platform's most precious resource. Message them too often and they mute you — then nobody comes when it truly matters. So Raktify never broadcasts. It builds a shortlist of at most 20 people, chosen by six checks, and messages only them. If they don't respond, the next 20. And before anything fires, the coordinator gets a heads-up with the power to pause.

One line to remember: spend donors like money — carefully, and only on real needs. Hands over to: Flow 5 (the message and the yes). Falls back to: Flow 8 if the shortlist is empty.

Flow 5 — The donor says yes What does the donor actually see, and what happens next?

The donor gets one WhatsApp message: which blood is needed, at which blood bank, and how far that is from where they are standing. One tap opens a page — no app, no password, no OTP. They pick the blood bank they'll visit, and that bank's screen immediately shows "a donor is on the way".

DetailWhy it's designed this way
No login on the alert pageA 45-year-old farmer at a bus stop will not remember a password. The link itself is the key — personal, time-limited, single-purpose.
Distance from current location, not home villagePeople travel. What matters is how far they are right now.
"No stock" banks still appear in the listAn empty fridge is exactly where a fresh donation helps most.
48-hour arrival windowEnough for a working person to plan; short enough that the patient isn't left waiting on a promise nobody will keep. After 48 h it's marked no-show and the next donors are called.
The patient's name is never shownPrivacy both ways: donors never see patient details; hospitals never see donor phone numbers.
One line to remember: one tap for the donor, full visibility for the bank. Hands over to: Flow 6 (arm to bag) — or loops back to Flow 4 / escalates to Flow 8.

Flow 6 — From arm to usable bag Why can't donated blood be used immediately?

Blood from an arm is never given straight to a patient. Every single bag — whether from an alert donor, a camp, or a walk-in — passes the same gate: quarantine, then five disease tests, then a second person's sign-off if anything looks wrong. This gate is also the exact moment an unverified person becomes a real donor (door ②). The rules live inside the database itself, so no software bug and no rushed staff member can skip them.

One line to remember: no test, no blood — no exceptions, not even in an emergency. Hands over to: Flow 7 (the bag travels) and back to Flow 1 (a newly verified donor joins the pool).

Flow 7 — Reaching the patient The last kilometre — and the only real finish line

A cleared bag is set aside for this specific patient, sent to the hospital, and checked one final time at the bedside — a crossmatch, mixing a drop of the patient's blood with the bag to prove they agree. Only then is it transfused. That transfusion is the platform's only definition of success.

One line to remember: the request is not "done" when blood is found — only when it is inside the patient. Ends the journey that began in Flow 2.

Flow 8 — When things don't go smoothly What if nobody responds? What if plans change?

Real life is messy: donors don't show up, requests get cancelled, bags expire. Raktify has a safety net for each. Two kinds: the widening circle (find help further away) and the janitors (automatic clean-up jobs that run day and night).

Flow 9 — Camps Filling the tank before the emergency

Everything above is demand-side: a patient needs blood now. Camps are the supply-side: collect many units on a good day, so that on a bad day the answer is already sitting in a fridge. Camps also bring in new donors — someone who donates at a camp walks out as a verified donor without ever visiting a website first.

Operations note: today, the blood bank's team + cold-chain availability for a camp date is confirmed offline before approval. Routing that confirmation through the platform itself is a planned next-phase feature.
One line to remember: camps are savings; emergencies are withdrawals. Feeds: Flow 1 (new donors) and Flow 3 (stocked fridges).
